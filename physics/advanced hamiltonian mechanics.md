# Advanced Hamiltonian Mechanics

[TOC]

## Purpose

Advanced Hamiltonian mechanics studies dynamics as geometry on phase space. Its central objects are the symplectic form, canonical transformations, Hamilton-Jacobi theory, action-angle variables, integrability, perturbation theory, and constrained systems.

This page expands the short overview in [basic principles of mechanics](./basic%20principles%20of%20mechanics.md).

## Symplectic Geometry

### Canonical one-form and symplectic two-form

On the cotangent bundle $T^*Q$ of configuration space $Q$, use canonical coordinates $(q^i,p_i)$. The canonical one-form is

$$
\theta = p_i\,\mathrm d q^i .
$$

The symplectic form is

$$
\omega = -\mathrm d\theta = \mathrm d q^i\wedge \mathrm d p_i .
$$

A symplectic manifold is a pair $(M,\omega)$ where $\omega$ is:

- closed: $\mathrm d\omega=0$.
- non-degenerate: $\iota_X\omega=0$ implies $X=0$.

### Hamiltonian vector field

For a Hamiltonian $H:M\to\mathbb R$, the Hamiltonian vector field $X_H$ is defined by

$$
\iota_{X_H}\omega = \mathrm dH .
$$

In canonical coordinates,

$$
X_H =
\frac{\partial H}{\partial p_i}\frac{\partial}{\partial q^i}
-
\frac{\partial H}{\partial q^i}\frac{\partial}{\partial p_i}.
$$

The integral curves of $X_H$ are the physical trajectories.

### Poisson bracket from symplectic form

The Poisson bracket can be written geometrically as

$$
\{f,g\} = \omega(X_f,X_g).
$$

In canonical coordinates,

$$
\{f,g\} =
\frac{\partial f}{\partial q^i}\frac{\partial g}{\partial p_i}
-
\frac{\partial f}{\partial p_i}\frac{\partial g}{\partial q^i}.
$$

The Jacobi identity

$$
\{f,\{g,h\}\}+\{g,\{h,f\}\}+\{h,\{f,g\}\}=0
$$

is the algebraic shadow of $\mathrm d\omega=0$.

## Canonical Transformations

### Definition

A transformation $(q,p)\mapsto(Q,P)$ is canonical if it preserves the symplectic form:

$$
\sum_i \mathrm d q^i\wedge \mathrm d p_i
=
\sum_i \mathrm d Q^i\wedge \mathrm d P_i .
$$

Equivalently, it preserves the fundamental Poisson brackets:

$$
\{Q^i,Q^j\}=0,\qquad
\{P_i,P_j\}=0,\qquad
\{Q^i,P_j\}=\delta^i_j.
$$

### Generating functions

Canonical transformations can be generated by functions. Common types are:

$$
\begin{array}{c|c|c}
\text{Generating function} & \text{Relations} & \text{New Hamiltonian}\\
\hline
F_1(q,Q,t) &
p_i=\partial F_1/\partial q^i,\quad
P_i=-\partial F_1/\partial Q^i &
K=H+\partial F_1/\partial t\\
F_2(q,P,t) &
p_i=\partial F_2/\partial q^i,\quad
Q^i=\partial F_2/\partial P_i &
K=H+\partial F_2/\partial t\\
F_3(p,Q,t) &
q^i=-\partial F_3/\partial p_i,\quad
P_i=-\partial F_3/\partial Q^i &
K=H+\partial F_3/\partial t\\
F_4(p,P,t) &
q^i=-\partial F_4/\partial p_i,\quad
Q^i=\partial F_4/\partial P_i &
K=H+\partial F_4/\partial t
\end{array}
$$

### Infinitesimal canonical transformations

For a generator $G(q,p,t)$ and small parameter $\epsilon$,

$$
\delta q^i = \epsilon\{q^i,G\},\qquad
\delta p_i = \epsilon\{p_i,G\}.
$$

Examples:

- $G=H$ generates time evolution.
- $G=p_i$ generates translation of $q^i$.
- $G=L_i$ generates spatial rotations.

This is the Hamiltonian form of the connection between symmetry and conserved quantities.

## Hamilton-Jacobi Theory

### Principal function

Hamilton-Jacobi theory searches for a generating function $S(q,\alpha,t)$ that transforms the system to constants $(\alpha,\beta)$. It satisfies

$$
H\left(q,\frac{\partial S}{\partial q},t\right)
+\frac{\partial S}{\partial t}=0 .
$$

The canonical momentum is recovered from

$$
p_i = \frac{\partial S}{\partial q^i}.
$$

If a complete integral $S(q,\alpha,t)$ is known, then

$$
\beta_i=\frac{\partial S}{\partial \alpha_i}
$$

gives the remaining constants of motion.

### Time-independent Hamiltonian

When $H$ does not depend explicitly on time,

$$
S(q,t)=W(q)-Et,
$$

and

$$
H\left(q,\frac{\partial W}{\partial q}\right)=E.
$$

$W$ is Hamilton's characteristic function.

### Relation to optics and semiclassics

The Hamilton-Jacobi equation has the same structure as the eikonal equation in geometrical optics. In semiclassical quantum mechanics, the wave function often has the WKB form

$$
\psi(q)\sim A(q)e^{\frac{i}{\hbar}S(q)}.
$$

The leading order in $\hbar$ gives the Hamilton-Jacobi equation.

## Action-Angle Variables

### Definition

For periodic motion, define the action variable

$$
I = \frac{1}{2\pi}\oint p\,\mathrm d q .
$$

For $s$ degrees of freedom and invariant tori $T^s$, define

$$
I_i = \frac{1}{2\pi}\oint_{\gamma_i} p_j\,\mathrm d q^j .
$$

The conjugate variables $\theta^i$ are angle variables with period $2\pi$.

### Dynamics

In action-angle coordinates,

$$
H=H(I),\qquad
\dot I_i=0,\qquad
\dot\theta^i=\frac{\partial H}{\partial I_i}=\omega^i(I).
$$

The motion is linear on a torus:

$$
\theta^i(t)=\theta^i(0)+\omega^i(I)t .
$$

### Example: harmonic oscillator

For

$$
H=\frac{p^2}{2m}+\frac12m\omega^2 q^2,
$$

the action is

$$
I=\frac{E}{\omega},
$$

and the Hamiltonian becomes

$$
H(I)=\omega I.
$$

## Integrable Systems

### Liouville integrability

A Hamiltonian system with $s$ degrees of freedom is completely integrable if there exist $s$ independent conserved quantities $F_1,\dots,F_s$ such that

$$
\{F_i,F_j\}=0 .
$$

The first integral is often the Hamiltonian itself, $F_1=H$.

### Liouville-Arnold theorem

If the common level set

$$
F_i(q,p)=c_i
$$

is compact and connected, then it is an $s$-dimensional torus, and the motion is quasi-periodic in action-angle variables.

### Examples

- One-dimensional bound motion.
- Central-force motion with conserved energy and angular momentum.
- Harmonic oscillator.
- Kepler problem.
- Free rigid body.

## Perturbation Theory and KAM Theory

Consider a nearly integrable Hamiltonian

$$
H(I,\theta)=H_0(I)+\epsilon H_1(I,\theta).
$$

Canonical perturbation theory tries to remove angle dependence order by order in $\epsilon$. Resonances occur when

$$
n_i\omega^i(I)=0
$$

for a nonzero integer vector $n$.

KAM theory states that, under suitable smoothness and non-degeneracy assumptions, many invariant tori of a non-resonant integrable system survive small perturbations. Destroyed resonant tori create island chains, separatrices, and chaotic layers.

## Constrained Systems

### Singular Lagrangians

If the Hessian

$$
W_{ij}=\frac{\partial^2L}{\partial\dot q^i\partial\dot q^j}
$$

is singular, the Legendre transform is not invertible. This produces primary constraints

$$
\phi_a(q,p)=0.
$$

### Total Hamiltonian

The total Hamiltonian is

$$
H_T=H_c+u^a\phi_a,
$$

where $H_c$ is the canonical Hamiltonian and $u^a$ are arbitrary multipliers.

Consistency requires that constraints remain true in time:

$$
\dot\phi_a=\{\phi_a,H_T\}\approx 0.
$$

This may determine multipliers or generate secondary constraints.

### First-class and second-class constraints

A constraint $\phi_a$ is first-class if

$$
\{\phi_a,\phi_b\}\approx 0
$$

for all constraints. First-class constraints generate gauge transformations.

Second-class constraints have an invertible constraint matrix

$$
C_{ab}=\{\chi_a,\chi_b\}.
$$

They are handled with the Dirac bracket:

$$
\{f,g\}_D
=
\{f,g\}
-
\{f,\chi_a\}C^{ab}\{\chi_b,g\}.
$$

### Degrees of freedom

If phase space has dimension $2N$, with $F$ first-class constraints and $S$ second-class constraints, then the number of physical configuration-space degrees of freedom is

$$
N_{\mathrm{phys}} = N - F - \frac{S}{2}.
$$

This machinery is essential for gauge theory, electromagnetism, Yang-Mills theory, and general relativity.
